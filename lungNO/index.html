Abstract

Diagnostic lung ultrasound (LUS) is common in clinics in the evaluation of the lungs for many different abnormalities and diseases. Ultrasound machine emits diagnostic pulse, receives pressure waves at the transducer surface, transforms them into radio frequency (RF) data and beamforms them to B-mode images, which are read by radiologists for diagnostic purposes and monitoring of lung disease. However, interpreting LUS B-mode images is challenging due to the ill- posedness of the problem, which comes from ultrasoundâ€™s inability to penetrate air and the complex high-order nonlinear physics of wave propagation in the lung. The interpretation difficulty consequently reduces the repeatability and increases the variance in LUS-based diagnosis and monitoring. In this work, we directly solve the inverse problem of ultrasound propagation in the lung and reconstruct lung aeration maps (the source) directly from the ultrasound RF data, which avoids the indirect and challenging interpretation of the B-mode images. Since an optimization-based solver is infeasible due to the high nonlinearity and extraordi- nary amount of time needed, we develop a deep learning based neural operator framework to approximate the inverse operator. Our model is based on the Fourier neural operator and parameterizes in Fourier space, enabling efficient RF data processing and reconstruct parameter maps of the lung ultrasound propagation model the inverse problem of ultrasound wave propagation. Lung percent aeration, an important clinical outcome, can be calculated from the reconstructed lung aeration map, unlike in semi-quantitative and human reader-dependent modern LUS scoring, and potentially can be used for diagnostics and monitoring. Notably, our model, mainly trained on simulated data, demonstrates robust performance in real-world RF data collected via ex-vivo swine lung scanning, with less than 10% percent aeration estimation error. As a pilot work that reconstructs lung aeration maps from ultrasound RF data, our model, designed specifically for the lung and its complex reverberation physics, unleashes the potential to further improve clinical interpretability, reproducibility and diagnostic capabilities.
